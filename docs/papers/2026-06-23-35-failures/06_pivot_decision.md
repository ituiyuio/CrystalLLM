# §4 The CWF Pivot: From Patching to Redesign

After 30 rounds of CMT failure, we faced a decision: continue patching, or pivot to a fundamentally different approach. We chose the latter. This section documents the pivot decision, the Closure Conjecture that motivated it, and the six-component architecture we designed as a candidate closed continuous model.

## 4.1 Why Patchwork Was a Dead End (Recap)

The CMT Campaign (§3) established that no combination of KAN FFNs, complex-valued attention, and Lie group position encodings could be added to a standard autoregressive Transformer without catastrophic failure. We documented five distinct failure modes (§A), each reproducible across multiple experiments:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Training-inference distribution drift}: any change to the training input distribution causes OOD failure at inference.
    \item \textbf{Closure violation}: discrete-continuous interfaces create information bottlenecks.
    \item \textbf{Architectural absence of ODE evolution}: even successful learning fails on chaotic dynamics tasks.
    \item \textbf{Tokenization bottleneck on continuous data}: VQ-style discrete codes cannot represent continuous orbits.
    \item \textbf{Optimizer-landscape mismatch on complex losses}: standard AdamW cannot navigate complex-Wirtinger loss surfaces.
\end{enumerate}

Each failure mode is structural, not implementation-specific. After 30 rounds of fixes, we had to conclude: \emph{the patching approach is exhausted}.

## 4.2 The Pivot Decision

In May 2026, we made the decision to abandon patching and design a continuous architecture from scratch. The pivot was based on the following reasoning:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The closure property had emerged as the key theoretical concept (failure mode 2). Continuous architectures must be \emph{closed} to maintain their representation throughout the network.

    \item No existing architecture is closed on a continuous representation space. Transformers are closed on $\mathbb{R}^d$ (trivially) but not on $\mathbb{D}^d$. Mamba and SSMs are closed on a continuous state but use linear dynamics that may not be expressive enough.

    \item A purpose-built closed architecture might avoid the CMT failure modes by construction, not by patching.
\end{itemize}

We formalized this as the \textbf{Closure Conjecture} and designed the \textbf{Closed Waveformer (CWF)} as a candidate architecture.

## 4.3 The Closure Conjecture

\begin{conjecture}[CWF Closure Conjecture]
Let $\psi \in \mathbb{D}^d \subset \mathbb{C}^d$ be a wave function state in the open unit disk. Let $\mathcal{F}_l: \mathbb{D}^d \to \mathbb{D}^d$ be any of the six component operators defined in §4.4. Then:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\mathcal{F}_l$ is well-defined (maps into the open disk).
    \item $\mathcal{F}_l$ is a smooth map (infinitely differentiable).
    \item The composition $\mathcal{F}_N \circ \cdots \circ \mathcal{F}_1$ preserves $\|\psi\| < 1$.
    \item The gradient $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ exists and is bounded for any reasonable loss $\mathcal{L}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{conjecture}

If this conjecture holds, we have a well-defined continuous-time language model that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Has no discrete token interface internally.
    \item Can be trained via standard gradient methods.
    \item Has well-defined information flow (no bottleneck at the discrete-continuous interface).
\end{itemize}

If the conjecture fails, continuous architectures cannot exist in the proposed form.

\section{4.4 The Six-Component Closed Architecture}

We designed the CWF as a stack of six closed components. Each component must satisfy the closure property: $\mathcal{F}: \mathbb{D}^d \to \mathbb{D}^d$.

\subsection{Component 1: Continuous Input Encoding}

The CWF must accept continuous inputs, not discrete tokens. Candidate designs:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{(A) Frequency-domain embedding}: $\mathcal{E}(x) = \mathrm{FFT}(x) / \|x\|_2$. Natural for periodic signals, clean closure.
    \item \textbf{(B) Sparse dictionary encoding}: $\mathcal{E}(x) = D\alpha$ with $\ell_2$ normalization.
    \item \textbf{(C) Neural encoder with closure loss}: any encoder with $\|\mathcal{E}(x)\|^2 < 1 - \epsilon$.
\end{itemize}

We selected (A) for the prototype due to its mathematical cleanliness and absence of additional trainable parameters.

\subsection{Component 2: Lie Group Position Rotation}

Position encoding is integrated via a context-dependent Lie algebra element:
$$\mathcal{P}_\theta(\psi) = \exp(A(\psi)) \cdot \psi$$
where $A(\psi) \in \mathfrak{so}(d)$ is a skew-symmetric matrix generated by a small network from the current state. The matrix exponential $\exp: \mathfrak{so}(d) \to SO(d)$ is an isometry, so $\|\mathcal{P}_\theta(\psi)\| = \|\psi\|$. Closure is automatic.

For computational efficiency, we use the Cayley transform instead of the matrix exponential:
$$R = (I + A/2)(I - A/2)^{-1}$$
which has the same closure property but requires only one matrix inversion.

\subsection{Component 3: Complex-Valued Attention}

Standard softmax attention is replaced with complex interference:
$$\mathcal{A}_\theta(\psi)_i = \sum_j \frac{\langle \phi_i, \psi_j \rangle}{|\langle \phi_i, \psi_j \rangle| + \epsilon} \cdot V_j$$

The attention weights are complex unit-magnitude (after the normalization), preserving phase information. The output is normalized post-attention to maintain closure.

Critical difference from softmax: the normalization is unit-phase (preserves phase), not exponential dominance (collapses to mode).

\subsection{Component 4: Complex KAN-FFN}

The FFN uses continuous basis functions on the complex manifold:
$$\mathcal{K}_\theta(\psi) = \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k \cdot B_k(\psi)$$

where $B_k$ are B-spline basis functions (or Siren sinusoidal activations for the prototype). Spectral norm constraints on $\alpha_k$ maintain bounded output.

\subsection{Component 5: Born-Stable Normalization}

After every layer, the state is projected back to $\mathbb{D}^d$:
$$\mathcal{N}(\psi) = \frac{\psi}{\max(\|\psi\|, 1 - \epsilon)}$$

This is a hard projection, not a soft rescaling. It guarantees $\|\psi\| \leq 1 - \epsilon < 1$ at every step.

\subsection{Component 6: Output via Born Rule}

At the final layer only, the wave state is decoded via the Born rule:
$$P(v) = \frac{|\langle \Phi_v, \psi_{\mathrm{final}} \rangle|^2}{\sum_v |\langle \Phi_v, \psi_{\mathrm{final}} \rangle|^2}$$

The probability distribution is used for loss computation and human display. Crucially, the next training step uses $\psi_{\mathrm{final}}$ directly (continuous state), not $\mathrm{argmax}\,P(v)$ (discrete sample).

\section{4.5 The Prototype (Phase 0)}

We implemented a minimal CWF prototype with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Input: continuous real-valued sequence $x_{1:S}$
    \item Internal state: $\psi \in \mathbb{D}^d$ ($d = 32$ in the prototype)
    \item Single block: Components 2--5 stacked once
    \item Output: continuous real-valued prediction $\hat{y}$
    \item Loss: MSE on continuous predictions
\end{itemize}

The prototype was tested on a synthetic harmonic sequence task: predict $y_{t+1}$ given $y_{1:t}$ where $y_t = \sin(\omega t + \phi)$.

\textbf{Results}: the prototype maintained closure throughout training (max $\psi$ norm $< 1$ across 10 random batches). It learned the task: training loss decreased from 320 to 3 over 2000 steps. It was 65$\times$ slower than the AR baseline (complex-valued operations are unoptimized). The relative loss was $2.5\times$ the AR baseline at 500 steps, but CWF converged faster (90 vs 190 steps).

The Phase 0 prototype demonstrated that closure is achievable. It did not demonstrate that closure is sufficient for chaotic dynamics tasks. The Phase 1 experiment (§6) tested this harder question.

\section{4.6 The Three Critical Conjectures

Before any large-scale engineering investment, we identified three conjectures that must be addressed (proof or counter-example):

\subsection{C1: Convergence}
Gradient descent on CWF with any reasonable loss converges to a local optimum. \emph{Risk}: complex Wirtinger derivatives have non-trivial interaction with non-convex loss landscapes.

\subsection{C2: Isometric Embedding}
There exists a low-distortion encoder $\mathcal{E}: \mathrm{text} \to \mathbb{D}^d$ such that semantic similarity in text space is preserved by inner product in $\mathbb{D}^d$. \emph{Risk}: text is fundamentally discrete; the encoder may destroy information at the discrete-continuous interface.

\subsection{C3: Gradient Stability}
Wirtinger gradient of CWF loss is numerically stable in FP32 (no explosion, no vanishing) for sequences up to length 1024. \emph{Risk}: complex gradients can have phase cancellations that lead to silent underflow.

None of these conjectures was verified in the Phase 0 prototype. Phase 1 (§6) tested C1 implicitly (by attempting training) and found partial support (loss decreased without explosion) but full convergence was not achieved.

\section{4.7 Why Phase 1 Was the Right Next Step

The pivot to CWF was motivated by the failure of patching, but the pivot itself could not validate the CWF hypothesis. Phase 1 was designed as the \emph{GO/NO-GO gate}: if CWF could learn chaotic dynamics (the Lorenz system), the CWF Manifesto hypothesis would be supported. If not, the CWF path would also be falsified, completing the systematic elimination of "patching continuity into autoregressive Transformers" approaches.

The Phase 1 result is the subject of §6.
